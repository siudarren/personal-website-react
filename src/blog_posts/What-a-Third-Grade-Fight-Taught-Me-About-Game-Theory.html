What a Third-Grade Fight Taught Me About Game Theory

May 4th, 2025

Reading time: 5 minutes

Back in middle school, I read an ancient Chinese short story called "Tian Ji's Horse Racing" (田忌賽馬). It tells of a horse race between Tian Ji, a military general from the state of Qi, and the King of Qi. Both men enjoyed racing and frequently bet against each other. Each would select three horses, ranking them as best, second-best, and worst. Horses of matching ranks competed against one another, and whoever won two out of three races was declared the winner.

However, Tian Ji's horses were not as fast as the king's. If he raced his horses according to their true performance, he would lose all three matches. With guidance from his strategist, Sun Bin, Tian Ji devised a clever plan: he labeled his slowest horse as his best, his fastest horse as second-best, and his second-best horse as the worst.

Using this tactic, Tian Ji deliberately sacrificed the race against the king's best horse to maximize his chances of winning the other two — and ultimately, the match itself. In the story, Tian Ji's strategy succeeded: he won the race, and the impressed King of Qi became a student of Sun Bin.

Although the story ends here, there's more to explore about the strategy. As brilliant as it was, Tian Ji's approach would likely only work once. His advantage depended on knowing the exact order of the king's horses. To counter this, the king could simply randomize his lineup, making it impossible for Tian Ji to plan his matchups in advance. At that point, the race would shift from a simple contest of speed to a complex problem of game theory.

To better understand the setup, we can think of the horses as numbers and the races as simple comparisons: the greater the number, the faster the horse. Suppose the king's horses have speeds of 2, 4, and 6, while Tian Ji's horses have speeds of 1, 3, and 5. Both the king and Tian Ji have 3!=6 possible ways to order their horses.

To prevent Tian Ji from planning his lineup strategically, the king could randomly choose one of the six possible orders, giving each order an equal probability of 1/6. Tian Ji, with no information about the king's arrangement, would also have to randomly pick an order, hoping that luck would be on his side — and that he would happen to choose the only lineup capable of countering the king's horses, which is a chance of 1/6.

Since both Tian Ji and the king have six possible ways to arrange their horses, there are 6×6=36 possible matchup combinations in total. Out of these 36 possible outcomes, Tian Ji would win only 6 matchups, giving him a 1/6 chance of winning. Welp, a one-in-six chance is still better than no chance at all, right?

Although in life, generalizing groups or problems is usually a bad idea — both morally and practically — we mathematicians (self-proclaimed, of course) can't help but love generalizing problems! What are the odds of Tian Ji winning if there are n horses on each side?

You might have noticed from the previous section that the exact order of the king's horses isn't all that important. Since the matchups are relative, and we care more about how the horses are paired against each other, we can simplify the problem: given a fixed lineup of the king's horses, how many different ways can Tian Ji arrange his horses to win?

Without loss of generality, we can assume that the king orders his horses from slowest to fastest, following the sequence 2, 4, 6, …, 2n - 2, 2n (meaning the first horse has a speed of 2, the second has a speed of 4, and so on). For Tian Ji's m-th fastest horse, which has a speed of 2m - 1, to win its matchup, it must compete against one of the king's first m-1 horses — that is, a horse slower than his.

At this point, we have transformed the problem into one of combinatorics. For Tian Ji to win the overall match, more than half of his horses must be assigned to matchups where their opponent's position number is less than their own ranking among Tian Ji's horses. In other words, Tian Ji's m-th fastest horse must be paired against one of the king's first m-1 horses (e.g., his 3rd fastest horse must face one of the king's first two horses).

In combinatorics, we refer to such favorable matchups as exceedances — positions where the value (here, Tian Ji's ranking) exceeds its index (the position in the king's lineup). The number of permutations of n elements with exactly k exceedances is given by the Eulerian numbers. Using this, we can calculate how many ways Tian Ji can order his horses so that more than half of them are in matchups where they exceed their opponent's position — giving him a winning outcome.

Eulerian Numbers:

$$ A(n,k) = \displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{k}(-1)^i\binom{n+1}{i}(k+1-i)^n $$

By summing up all the Eulerian Numbers we can get the equation for the possibility of winning:

$$ P_n = \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{k=\lfloor n/2 \rfloor + 1}^{n} \sum_{j=0}^{k+1} (-1)^j \binom{n+1}{j} (k + 1 - j)^n $$

Here, we can see that as n increases — that is, as more horses are included in the competition — Tian Ji's chances of winning also improve. In fact, as n→∞, the probability of Tian Ji winning approaches 1/2. So, if we were in Tian Ji's position, facing a challenge from the king, our best strategy would be to advocate for a race involving as many horses as possible.

At this point, you might be thinking I tricked you into reading a whole paragraph about game theory and math — and that it has absolutely nothing to do with the fight I had in third grade. But hang in there — that fight is where everything starts to make sense.

Back in third grade, I got into a fight with a classmate.

I called in my sixth-grade older brother.

He called his eighth-grade brother.

So I brought in my cousin, who was a high school freshman.

Then he called his brother — a senior in high school!

At that point, we thought we were doomed...

Luckily, my cousin had already learned about Tian Ji's Horse Racing in class — and in the end, I went up against his high school senior brother.
