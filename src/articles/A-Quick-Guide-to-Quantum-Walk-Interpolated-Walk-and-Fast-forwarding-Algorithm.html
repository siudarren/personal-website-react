A Quick Guide to Quantum Walk, Interpolated Walk, and Fast-forwarding Algorithm

May 6th, 2024

Reading time: 20 minutes

Abstract

Quantum walks, distinguished by unique properties not present in classical random walks, facilitate the development of algorithms that achieve quantum speedup. This paper delves into the Fast-forwarding Algorithm by Ambainis et al., which assures a quadratic acceleration compared to its classical counterpart in locating marked vertices within arbitrary graphs. Furthermore, this study applies the Fast-forwarding Algorithm to ascertain an upper bound on the computational cost necessary to confirm matrix multiplication A⁢B=C for matrices A,B,C∈ℝn×n, with a tolerance of ϵ.

1 Introduction

Quantum walks are an emerging field within the realm of quantum computing and have significant implications across various scientific disciplines. The two primary models of quantum walks are the discrete and the continuous quantum walks [VA12]. In this paper, we will discuss the properties of both walks and demonstrate how one can simulate continuous-time quantum walk with discrete quantum walk with method provided by [C09]. The simplest form of a discrete quantum walk can be realized through two quantum systems: a coin and a walker [VA12]. We will use the basic example of a quantum walk on an unrestricted line to illustrate the differences in spatial evolution between a quantum walk and a classical walk.

We will then introduce the concept of the interpolated walk, a variation of the quantum walk devised by Krovi et al. for identifying marked vertices in specific graphs. If the walker arrives at a marked vertex, there is a probability s∈[0,1) that the walker will remain at the same vertex without proceeding to the next vertices. The weight s, when applied to the system, ensures that, given sufficient time, the system will converge to a uniform superposition of marked vertices, thus guaranteeing the detection of a marked vertex upon measurement [KMOR15].

The average number of steps required for a classical random walk to find a marked vertex is called the hitting time, denoted by H⁢T. The algorithm proposed by Krovi et al. guarantees a quadratic speedup for graphs with only one marked vertex, reducing the hitting time to 𝒪⁢(H⁢T) [KMOR15]. However, when multiple marked vertices are present in the graph, the algorithm can only provide a quadratic speedup relative to the extended hitting time, denoted by H⁢T+, with hitting time 𝒪⁢(H⁢T+) [AGJK20]. In some cases, the extended hitting time can be significantly greater than the classical hitting time. This method is not optimal for identifying a single marked vertex when multiple marked vertices are present within the system and are distributed in such a manner that prevents even probability distribution among them [AGJK20]. In Section 4, we will provide an example where H⁢T+≫H⁢T.

To address this issue, Ambainis et al. introduced the usage of fast-forwarding algorithm onto interpolation walk. The core concept of the fast-forwarding algorithm is to implement t steps of the walk while only using the walking operator t times. This technique ensures a quadratic speedup in finding a marked vertex on any graph. We will then employ the results from Ambainis to establish an upper bound for the computational cost of verifying the matrix equation A⁢B=C for matrices A,B,C∈ℝn×n, within an error margin of ϵ [AGJK20].

In this paper, our primary aim is to provide intuitive insights into understanding these algorithms. We will delve into quantum walks, beginning with the foundational concept of a quantum walk on an infinite, unrestricted line, and contrast it with the classical random walk. Following this, we will discuss the interpolated quantum walk as introduced by Krovi et al., and provide a scenario when the performance of the interpolated walk is significantly inferior to that of the classical walk. Then we present the Fast-forwarding Algorithm, a significant advancement by Ambainis et al., which facilitates a quantum speedup in a locating marked vertex on any graph. Lastly, we apply the Ambainis et al. algorithm to deduce an upper bound for the computational cost of the problem A⁢B=C.

2 Quantum walk on a line

There are many distinct and intriguing properties of quantum walks that differ significantly from those of classical random walks. In this section, we will compare the most basic example of a quantum walk, discrete quantum walk on an unrestrictedly line (DQWL), to its classical counterpart. For further knowledge about the properties of quantum walks, Quantum Walks: A Comprehensive Review by Salvador Elías Venegas-Andraca is an excellent resource to begin with.

2.1 Structure of DQWL

To build a Discrete Quantum Walk on a Line (DQWL), we need a coin, a walker, evolution operators for each, and a measurement oracle [VA12].

Coin: The coin is a quantum system in a two-dimensional Hilbert space, ℋC, with canonical basis states |0⟩ and |1⟩.

Walker: The walker is a quantum system in a countably infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, ℋW. The system can be represented as ∑iai⁢|i⟩W where |i⟩W are the position states and ∑i|ai|2=1. The initial state of the walker is usually set to |position initial⟩=|0⟩W.

The space that holds the total system can be expressed as the tensor product of the coin and walker spaces: ℋt=ℋC⊗ℋW.

Evolution Operator for Coin: The evolution operator of the coin, which flips the coin when applied, can be represented using the Hadamard operator. The Hadamard operator is defined as:

H^=12⁢(|0⟩C⁢⟨0|+|0⟩C⁢⟨1|+|1⟩C⁢⟨0|−|1⟩C⁢⟨1|) (1)

Evolution Operator for Walker: The evolution operator for the walker is a conditional shift operator. It increases the position state from |i⟩W to |i+1⟩W if the coin is in the |0⟩C state, and decreases the position state from |i⟩W to |i−1⟩W if the coin is in the |1⟩C state. The operator is given by:

S^=|0⟩C⁢⟨0|⊗∑i|i+1⟩W⁢⟨i|+|1⟩C⁢⟨1|⊗∑i|i−1⟩W⁢⟨i| (2)

Evolution Operator: The evolution operator for the entire system can be written as the combination of the two operators above. We first apply H^ to flip the coin, then shift the position of the walker according to the result of the coin flip.

U^=S^⋅(H^⊗𝕀W) (3)

The state of the system after t steps can be expressed as:

|ψ⟩t=U^t⁢|ψ⟩initial (4)

where |ψ⟩initial=|coin⟩initial⊗|position⟩initial

2.2 Probability Distributions of DQWL

There are some interesting properties regarding the probability distributions of DQWL that differ from those of the classical random walk, the two most noticeable being 1) non-Gaussian and 2) skewed distributions. In the remaining part of this section, we will present simulations of DQWL using Python and analyze these properties.

Figure 1 shows the probability distribution of the walker’s position after 10 and 100 steps, starting from the initial state |ψ⟩initial=|0⟩C⊗|0⟩W. The figure is only showing the possibility amplitudes of even vertices, ignoring the odd vertices, since possibility of odd vertices must be 0 afte even steps. The distribution is noticeably skewed to the right, with increasing fluctuations on both ends. Unlike a classical random walk, the Discrete Quantum Walk on a Line (DQWL) does not follow a Gaussian distribution. Instead, the walker is more likely to explore further positions rather than remaining near the initial position.

The skewness of the distribution is attributable to the characteristics of the Hadamard operator. The negative sign in the Hadamard operator introduces constructive and destructive interference in the system, resulting in the biased distribution.

If we choose different initial coin states for the system, the resulting distribution will behave differently. Figure 2 displays the outcomes when the initial coin states |0⟩, |1⟩, and 12⁢(|0⟩+i⁢|1⟩) are used, and the simulation is run for 500 steps. The distribution with the initial coin state |1⟩ is an exact mirror image of that with the initial coin state |0⟩.

Moreover, setting the initial coin state to 12⁢(|0⟩+i⁢|1⟩) results in a symmetric distribution. These results demonstrate that the distribution can be manipulated by choosing appropriate initial states, indicating that the quantum walk is sensitive to the initial state of the system in a way that is distinctly different from a classical random walk.

3 Continuous-time Quantum Walk

Unlike classical random walks, where the limit of a discrete random walk can simply be taken to obtain a continuous random walk, the continuous-time quantum walk (CTQW) does not require a coin in the system, resulting in a smaller dimension compared to the discrete-time quantum walk.

In DTQW, the initial state is defined across two distinct spaces: the coin and position spaces. Consequently, the evolution involves the application of two unitary operations: the coin operator then the walker operator. For DTQW, the coin state is an essential part, controlling the evolution of the system in Hilbert space (HC⊗HW).

In CTQW, the evolution of a walker on a graph is governed by the Schrödinger equation, with the Hamiltonian typically defined as either the Laplacian or the adjacency matrix of the graph. Similar to continuous-time random walks, the vertices of the graph continuously exchange probabilities.

In the remaining part of this section, we briefly introduce a method provided by Childs in [C09] to simulate CTQW using DTQW.

3.1 Simulation of CTQW with DTQW

First, we start by showing how we can construct a corresponding DTQW to any Hermitian matrix. Then, we show that by using the correspondence, we can construct a DTQW that simulates any CTQW generated by H with appropriate limits.

For any Hermitian matrix H, let |j⟩ from j=1,2,…,N be the normal basis of H. The absolute value of H be defined as:

a⁢b⁢s⁢(H)=∑j,k=1N|Hj⁢k|⁢|j⟩⁢⟨k| (5)

The principal eigenvector of a⁢b⁢s⁢(H) be

|d⟩=dj⁢|j⟩,w⁢h⁢e⁢r⁢e⁢dj=⟨j|d⟩. (6)

Lastly, we define an orthonormal set of quantum states |ψ1⟩,…,|ψN⟩ defined on the tensor product space ℂN⊗ℂN as:

|ψj⟩=1‖a⁢b⁢s⁢(H)‖⁢∑k=1NHj⁢k∗⁢dkdj⁢|j,k⟩ (7)

here, we can understand |ψj⟩ as the superposition of all position state |k⟩ (k=1,…,N), that is directly connected to |j⟩, with |j⟩ attached as the second register. With this, we can define the isometry operator T^, that maps CTQW space to its DTQW counterpart, and U^, the walk operator.

T^=∑j=1N|ψj⟩⁢⟨j|,U^=i⁢S^⁢(2⁢T^⁢T^†−1). (8)

here, S^ is the swap operator, S^⁢|x,y⟩=|y,x⟩.

However, in [C09], it was shown that to obtain a good approximation of CTQW, we need to do two more things:

1. Rotate the basis by I+i⁢S^2

2. Make the fraction h=‖H‖‖a⁢b⁢s⁢(H)‖ arbitrary small.

This can be done by introducing the lazy walk: only take a step with probability ϵ<1. Here, the new orthogonal basis and isometry operator are

|ψjϵ⟩=ϵ⁢|ψj⟩+1−ϵ⁢|⊥j⟩,andT^ϵ=∑j=1N|ψjϵ⟩⁢⟨j|, (9)

Now, after defining all the notations, we can finally introduce the CTQW simulation algorithm:

Step 1: We first convert the basis state from that of the DTQW to the basis state of the lazy walk version of the CTQW using T^ϵ. Then, we rotate the basis by(1+i⁢S^)/2, setting up the initial state for DTQW.

Step 2: Analogous to applying the walk operator in DTQW, this step involves taking τ steps.

Step 3: This step involves measuring the probability, also in the basis of DTQW.

After making τ steps, the probability of being in a state |j⟩ is

P⁢r⁢(j)=|⟨j|⁢e−i⁢τ⁢ϵ⁢H^‖a⁢b⁢s⁢(H)‖⁢|ϕ0⟩|2 (10)

by setting ϵ=‖a⁢b⁢s⁢(H)‖⁢tτ, we get

P⁢r⁢(j)=|⟨j|⁢e−i⁢t⁢H^⁢|ϕ0⟩|2 (11)

which is the desired continuous-time quantum walk.

4 Interpolated Walk

The main idea of the interpolated walk is to introduce a variable, s, into the system. This variable represents the probability that, whenever the walker lands on a marked vertex, it will remain there in the next step. By incorporating this mechanism, the marked vertex gradually ’absorbs’ probability, leading to a distribution that is more evenly spread across the marked vertices [KMOR15]. In this chapter, we will discuss only ergodic Markov chains, which ensure that every state is reachable from every other state in a finite number of steps.

4.1 Semi-absorbing Markov Chains

Let X be the set of all vertices of the graph, M be the set of marked vertices, m≔|M| is the number of marked vertices, with m>0, and U=X/M.

We define the stochastic matrix P and P′ as

P≔(PU⁢UPU⁢MPM⁢UPM⁢M),P′≔(PU⁢UPU⁢M0I), (12)

Notice that P is the normal stochastic matrix with the marked vertices moved to the end of the matrix, and P′ is the stochastic matrix that permits movement only if the current vertex is not marked. Now we can define the interpolated matrix as follows:

P⁢(s)≔(1−s)⁢P+s⁢P′,0≤s≤1. (13)

If the current vertex is not marked, the walker will move around as usual. However, if the current vertex is marked, there is a probability s that the walker will remain in its current position [KMOR15].

4.2 Search Algorithms on Graph

In this part, we are going to explore the quantum search algorithm provided by [AGJK20]. Let us introduce some operations:

Check(M): Checks if a vertex is marked;

Setup(𝒫): Constructs the superposition |π⟩=∑x∈Xπx⁢|x⟩, here, π is the stationary distribution of 𝒫;

Update(𝒫): Performs one step in the process.

The implementation cost of each operation is denoted by C, S, and U respectively. Next, we will introduce Update(𝒫), which serves a role analogous to the evolution operator in a Discrete Quantum Walk on a Line (DQWL). The step unitary, V⁢(𝒫), is defined as

∀x∈X:V⁢(𝒫)⁢|0¯⟩⁢|x⟩=∑y∈XPx⁢y⁢|y,x⟩, (14)

where |0¯⟩ is some fixed reference point on the graph. We can understand V⁢(𝒫) as the operator that allows a step from the current vertex to all possible vertices in a superposition.

Next, we can define the quantum walk operator as

W⁢(P):=V†⁢(𝒫)⁢SHIFT⁢V⁢(𝒫)⁢REF. (15)

where SHIFT swaps the two register values |x,y⟩→|y,x⟩ and REF=(2⁢|0⟩⁢⟨0|−I)⊗I flips the state with respect to 0¯.

With an understanding of the normal quantum walk operator on graphs and the concept of interpolated walk, we can now define the interpolated quantum walk operator as:

W⁢(s):=V†⁢(𝒫,s)⁢SHIFT’⁢V⁢(𝒫,s)⁢REF′, (16)

where SHIFT′:=|0⟩⁢⟨0|⊗SHIFT+|1⟩⁢⟨1|⊗I, the new reflection operator is REF′:=(2⁢|0⟩⁢⟨0|−I)⊗I, and V⁢(P,s) for all x∈U acts as I⊗V⁢(P) on the initial state |0⟩⁢|0⟩⁢|x⟩, and for x∈M acts as |0⟩⁢|0⟩⁢|x⟩↦1−s⁢|0⟩⁢V⁢(P)⁢|0⟩⁢|x⟩+s⁢|1⟩⁢|0⟩⁢|x⟩.

4.3 Optimizing Interpolated Parameter s

Intuitively, we might think by setting s to 1 would increase the speed of the search, since our ultimate goal is to let the system approach the superposition of the marked vertices as fast as possible. However, this might not be the case. In [KMOR15], the author showed that every iteration of W⁢(P′) on |π⟩⁢|0¯⟩ may remain far from |M⟩⁢|0¯⟩.

They stated that the optimal value of the interpolated parameter s is instead related to pM≔∑x∈Mπx, which is the probability that an element is marked in the stationary distribution.

We define |vn⁢(s)⟩ as

|vn⁢(s)⟩=cos⁡θ⁢(s)⁢|U⟩+sin⁡θ⁢(s)⁢|M⟩, (17)

where

cos⁡θ⁢(s)=(1−s)⁢(1−pM)1−s⁢(1−pM),sin⁡θ⁢(s)=pM1−s⁢(1−pM) (18)

We can see that when s=0,

v⁢(s)=v⁢(0)=1−pM⁢|U⟩+pM⁢|M⟩=π (19)

where π is the stationary distribution of P, the initial state of the algorithm that we set up.
Figure 3: We want to find an s such that ⟨U|vn⁢(s)⟩=cos⁡θ⁢(s)≥ϵ1and ⟨M|vn⁢(s)⟩=sin⁡θ⁢(s)≥ϵ1.

From figure(4), we see that we start from |vn⁢(0)⟩ and we want to apply W⁢(s) on |vn⁢(0)⟩ to get |vn⁢(s)⟩ where |vn⁢(s)⟩ has a huge overlap with both |U⟩ and |M⟩. This is because we want to take minimal step to achieve a high possibility of measuring |M⟩ with the standard basis.

Now, we state Theorm 20 from [KMOR15]:
Theorem: Assume that the values of pM and H⁢T+⁢(𝒫,M) are known, and let s∈[0,1), T≥1, and 12≥ϵ1≥ϵ2≥0 be some parameters. If

cos⁡θ⁢(s)⁢sin⁡θ⁢(s)≥ϵ1a⁢n⁢dT≥π2⁢ϵ2⁢H⁢T⁢(s) (20)

then Search(P,M,s,[l⁢o⁢g⁢T]) finds a marked vertex with success probability at least

pM+(1−pM)⁢(ϵ1−ϵ2)2 (21)

Here, we can see that to increase the probability of measuring a marked vertex after log⁡T steps, we can either increase ϵ1 or decrease ϵ2. However, decreasing ϵ2 means that we will have to increase T due to Eq.20 which will increase the complexity. On the other hand, we can try to maximize ϵ1 by finding an optimal s for |vn⁢(s)⟩ (Note that increasing s will also increase H⁢T⁢(s) which leads to the increase of T).

The proof of the above theorem is first done by Szegedy and can be found in Appendix B.1 of [KMOR15].

[KMOR15] also showed that |vn⁢(s)⟩ and |vn⁢(s)⟩⁢|0¯⟩ are the principal eigenvectors of the interpolated discriminant matrix D⁢(s) and the walk operator W⁢(s) both with eigenvalues of 1. Furthermore, |vn⁢(s)⟩=|π⁢(s)T⟩ where |π⁢(s)⟩ is the stationary distribution of W⁢(s). Thus, by continuously apply W⁢(s) we are guarenteed to obtain |vn⁢(s)⟩.

In [AGJK20], it is conjectured that for any graph, there is always an optimal interpolated parameter s∈[0,1) and t=𝒪⁢(H⁢T) such that Algorithm 1 succeeds with a probability of Ω⁢(1). However, despite numerical experiments supporting the conjecture, there is yet mathematical proofs supporting the conjecture.

4.4 Classical Hitting Time

To understand how fast the quantum algorithm can accelerate processes, we first need to comprehend the classical hitting time to establish a basis for comparison.

The discriminant matrix, D⁢(𝒫), of a Markov chain 𝒫 is defined as

D⁢(𝒫)≔𝒫∘𝒫T, (22)

where ∘ represents the Hadamard product, and the square root is taken element-wise. The hitting time of Markov chain 𝒫 is then

H⁢T⁢(𝒫,M)=∑k=1n−|M||⟨ψk′|U⟩|21−λk′, (23)

where λk′ are the eigenvalues of the discriminant matrix D′=D⁢(P′) in nondecreasing order, |ψk′⟩ are the corresponding eigenvectors, and |U⟩ is the unit vector defined as

|U⟩:=11−pM⁢∑x∈Mπx⁢|x⟩,

where pM is the probability of drawing a marked vertex from the stationary distribution π of P [AGJK20].

4.5 Extended Hitting Time

When there is only one marked vertex, the quantum hitting time is quadratically faster than the classical hitting time (H⁢T). However, when there are more than one marked vertex, we can no longer compare the quantum hitting time directly to H⁢T. Instead, we must compare it to the extended hitting time defined in [KMOR15].

Let D⁢(s) be denoted as the discriminant matrix D⁢(P⁢(s)) where P⁢(s) is the interpolated matrix defined in Section 4.1. The interpolated hitting time, H⁢T⁢(s), is defined as

H⁢T⁢(s):=∑k=1n−1|⟨ψk⁢(s)|U⟩|21−λk⁢(s), (24)

where λk⁢(s) are the eigenvalues and |ψk⁢(s)⟩ are the eigenvectors of the discriminant matrix D⁢(s), and |U⟩ is the previously defined unit vector.

The extended hitting time is given by:

H⁢T+⁢(𝒫,M):=lims→1H⁢T⁢(s), (25)

It is important to note that when |M|=1, the extended hitting-time is equal to the classical hitting time H⁢T+⁢(𝒫,M)=H⁢T⁢(𝒫,M). And for some |M|>1 and 𝒫, H⁢T+⁢(𝒫,M)>H⁢T⁢(𝒫,M).

4.6 Runtime of Interpolated Walk

In [KMOR15], Krovi et al. provided an algorithm that guarantees to find a marked vertex with a runtime of 𝒪⁢(H⁢T+). This can be very useful when H⁢T+ is close to H⁢T. However, if H⁢T+≫H⁢T, then the quantum algorithm may be slower than its classical counterpart. Here, we will provide an example of such a case as presented in [AGJK20].

Consider a Markov chain represented by a N×N torus graph, where each vertex is connected to the four adjacent vertices and includes a self-loop. Let the marked vertices be the top left k×k section of the graph, and other marked vertices be located at coordinates (1+d×n,1+d×m), with n,m∈ℕ.

It is proved in [AGJK20] that for such a graph, H⁢T=𝒪⁢(d2⁢log⁡d) and H⁢T+=Ω⁢(N2). This discrepancy arises because the marked vertices are spread throughout the graph, with a maximum distance of d apart. As a result, it is relatively easy for a classical random walk to encounter one of the marked vertices. In contrast, the interpolated walk starts from a superposition of all unmarked vertices. Since it takes most unmarked vertices approximately N steps to reach the top-left corner, where they can be ’absorbed’ into the state, the hitting time becomes significantly larger and dependent on N rather than d.

5 Quantum walk Fast-forwarding

5.1 Intuitive Understanding

There is a lot of literature that has explained the mathematical proofs, showing the properties, limitations, and significant potential of Quantum Walk Fast-Forwarding (QFF), with my favorite being [as19]. Here, instead of restating all the mathematical proofs already published in existing papers, we will take a more intuitive approach to understanding QFF and its implications for interpolated walks.

In section 4.6, we have shown that although interpolated walks do work, the problem with them is that the algorithm is trying to solve a much harder problem than finding one marked vertex. So there are two ways to address this issue: 1) we can find an algorithm that is better suited to finding a single vertex, or 2) we stick with interpolated walks but try to perform them faster. Applying fast-forwarding is the latter option.

The easiest way to understand fast-forwarding is by considering it as a process where we aim to move t steps forward while only making t steps (or t calls to the update operation). This can be achieved by utilizing the Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU) technique [as19]. Consider the walk operator W as a block-encoding of the discriminant matrix D (recall D⁢(P)≔P∘PT and D⁢(P)=P when P is symmetric). We can then block-encode Dt with LCU by using D only ∝t times. The details of how to block-encode Dt, which involve using a Chebyshev expansion to approximate the Markov chain eigenvalue function, can be found in [as19]; however, these details will not be included here.

5.2 Upper Bound of Complexity

Let 𝒫 be any reversible Markov Chain on a finite state space X, and let M⊂X be a marked set. There is a quantum algorithm that. outputs a vertex x from M with bounded error in complexity

𝒪⁢(S⁢log⁡(H⁢T)+H⁢T⁢(U+C)⁢log⁡(H⁢T)⁢log⁡log⁡(H⁢T)) (26)

where HT is a known upper bound on H⁢T⁢(𝒫,M), S is the cost of the Setup(𝒫) operation, U is the cost of the Update(𝒫), operation, and C is the cost of the Check(M) operation [AGJK20].

6 Verifying Matrix Multiplication with QFF and Interpolated Walk

This section of the paper is inspired by Homework 3 from the class. Instead of utilizing Grover’s search algorithm to compare entries, we explore the application of quantum walks for this purpose.

Let A, B, and C be n×n matrices. We aim to find the complexity for verifying whether A⁢B=C using classical random walks and quantum walks.

6.1 Complexity with Classical Random Walk

Let us arbitrarily choose an entry in C and perform a classical random walk. At every vertex, we check whether the entry value matches that of A⁢B. It is helpful to visualize a matrix as a 2D torus, similar to the example from Section 4.6. From [LP17], we know that the hitting time for a classical random walk on a 2D torus is 𝒪⁢(n2⁢log⁡n). Additionally, the complexity of checking each entry is 𝒪⁢(n), making the total complexity 𝒪⁢(n3⁢log⁡n).

This approach is less efficient than calculating and comparing every entry, which has a complexity of 𝒪⁢(n3). However, let us now consider whether a quantum walk can provide a speedup.

6.2 Upper Bound of Quantum Walk

Since the algorithm requires that |M|=m>0, problem arises when AB=C since there won’t be any marked vertex. As a solution, we can arbitrarily choose an entry in C and change it to a number a, such that a≫x for all x∈A,B,C. If after measurement, the measured marked vertex is the one that we changed, we then change another vertex and make another measurement.

The probability of measuring the vertex that we changed is given by:

mn2⁢1m+n2−mn2⁢1m+1≈1m,(n2≫1) (27)

(there is a mn2 chance that we change a vertex that is already marked). Thus, if we run the algorithm k times and in each instance measure the vertex that we changed, the probability that A⁢B=C is true can be calculated as:

1−(1m)k, (28)

and since m is unknown, we consider the worst-case scenario where m=2. In this case, the probability that A⁢B=C after k measurements is:

1−(12)k. (29)

Now, we can finally start estimating the upper bound. From the operation cost C=𝒪⁢(n), Equation (26), and the classical hitting time given in [LP17], we can determine that an upper bound of the hitting time with a quantum walk is

𝒪⁢(n2⁢log⁡(n)⁢log⁡(n2⁢log⁡(n))⁢log⁡log⁡(n2⁢log⁡(n))) (30)

which asymptotically approximates to 𝒪⁢(n2).
This result is comparable to the fastest classical algorithm, Freivalds’ algorithm.

6.3 Conclusion

Although the algorithm is not superior in terms of complexity compared to the classical algorithm, it still holds an advantage. Using Freivalds’ algorithm, we can only detect whether A⁢B=C, but cannot directly identify the incorrect entry. In contrast, quantum walks allow us to collect this specific information.

This is because the quantum algorithm discussed here does not merely speed up Freivalds’ algorithm; rather, it accelerates the process of calculating all entries to find a marked vertex, which typically requires 𝒪⁢(n3).
