A Potentially Easier Way to Calculate the Cross Product

August 26th, 2024

Reading time: 3 minutes

I have to admit that the title is a bit of clickbait, as there isn't truly a 'better' way to approach mathematics. However, I still want to highlight an interesting aspect of how mathematical methods can vary across different regions.

One of the first things I noticed after arriving in the United States for college was how people write and calculate math differently. Due to variations in language, it is normal for people to write mathematical expressions in different orders. For example, when writing fractions, English speakers always write the numerator first, followed by the denominator. This is because in English,  2 3  is read as 'two over three,' so it is logical for them to do so. Conversely, Chinese speakers read it as 'three dividing the two', where the denominator precedes the numerator, leading them to write it accordingly.

I initially thought the difference in mathematical notation would be limited to elementary math and basic expressions, but I soon realized that even in higher-level college mathematics, people not only continue to write math differently but also calculate it differently.

When I took multivariable calculus as a freshman, I noticed that my American peers and professors calculated three-dimensional cross products by writing out a matrix. The first row contained the i, j, and k axes, the second row the first vector, and the third row the second vector.

[ i j k a b c d e f ]

The matrix representation of ( a , b , c ) × ( d , e , f ) .

Afterward, they compute the determinant of the matrix to obtain the cross product. (How people calculate determinants in different ways is also an interesting topic, but it's not our focus today, so I must regrettably skip it.)

| i j k a b c d e f | = i | b c e f | + j | a c d f | + k | a b d e | = ( b f - c e ) i + ( a f - c d ) j + ( a e - b d ) k

The determinant of the matrix.

On the other hand, in Asia, or at least in Taiwan, we don’t calculate the cross product as the determinant of a matrix. Instead, we write out the vector, starting with the second term, like this:

b c a b e f d e

The other representation of ( a , b , c ) × ( d , e , f ) .

Next, we calculate the cross product of adjacent numbers as if each group forms a two-dimensional matrix.

Each cross is a cross product

For each two-dimensional cross product we calculate, it will, surprisingly—or perhaps not—correspond to each coordinate of the cross product vector.

The two different methods, if examined closely, are essentially the same calculations. It's just that the Taiwanese method requires writing a lot less and does not require knowledge of 3D determinants. On the other hand, the American method highlights the connection between the cross product and matrices, which is a useful intuition when learning higher-level linear algebra and physics.

In my opinion, the difference may stem from the fact that in Taiwan, we are required to learn all the 'fancy' mathematical methods needed in physics early in high school, long before linear algebra is taught. Furthermore, it is no secret that in Asia, being good and fast at test-taking is more important than understanding the knowledge itself. Therefore, a faster but less intuitive method is chosen for teaching.
