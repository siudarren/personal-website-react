A Romantic Revisit to On Bullshit

May 16th, 2025

Reading time: 3 minutes

A few weeks ago, I went on a date with a girl.

She wasn't what I thought I was looking for — she didn't check many of the boxes I had kept in mind for a long-term partner — and yet, it turned out to be one of the best dates I've ever had. It wasn't romantic in any dramatic way. It was peaceful. We laughed, debated values, walked around, talked, and wandered into a few new stores and museums together — nothing particularly special. And yet, by the end, we both agreed it brought us a kind of ease we hadn't felt in a long time.

That date made me start to question everything I thought I was looking for in a partner. She didn't have a STEM degree. She wasn't the same age as me. She didn't come from a similar background. And none of that mattered.

It made me realize how many of my desires weren't really mine. They were absorbed — from expectations, from culture, from what other people told me would suit me.

The experience reminded me of a blog post my middle school English teacher once wrote, inspired by Harry Frankfurt's On Bullshit. He wrote about the bullshit flooding our lives, arguing that everyone he knew — himself included — bullshitted themselves into believing idiotic ideologies and chasing meaningless goals.

Bullshit is everywhere — from saying “great” when someone asks how you're doing on a terrible day, to cramming for a paper you don't care about, just to get a degree you don't care about, just to land a job that doesn't even care about your degree. We bullshit ourselves into thinking that a $300,000 college degree guarantees success, or that a high-paying, 90-hour workweek job will somehow lead to happiness.

I started to wonder: why did I want someone with a STEM degree in the first place? Was it because I thought it meant we'd have better conversations, or shared interests? If so, then why hadn't I been looking for that instead of the degree itself? A degree might hint at someone's passions or lifestyle, but it's no guarantee.

Degrees, age, race, occupation — all the labels I'd once used as filters — suddenly felt like distractions from what actually matters. Maybe I hadn't been looking for someone older, but for someone who's emotionally mature. Maybe it wasn't about finding a high-paying, “respectable” job, but about figuring out what kind of life I actually want to live.

Searching for what we truly want is difficult. It requires honesty with ourselves, ignoring what other people think of us (unless that's what we're actually searching for), questioning the desires we absorb from culture and society, and stepping outside our comfort zones to try different things. And perhaps, after all that effort, we still might never fully understand what we want.

But hey — isn't the process of searching already liberating?

I still don't know exactly what I want in a partner, or what kind of life I want to build — let alone how to find either. But that date gave me hope. Not just because it offered a few answers, but because it reminded me that when we let go of the bullshit and stay open to different possibilities, we start to hear what actually matters — not from others, but from ourselves.
