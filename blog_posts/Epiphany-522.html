Epiphany 522

June 2nd, 2025

Reading time: 4 minutes

Growing up in a family and community rooted in Chinese culture, I was always taught that everything has two sides. Just like Yin and Yang, nothing is purely good or purely bad: admiration and fame come with jealousy and betrayal; extraordinary talent comes with pressure; and success comes with loneliness. However, I had always thought those inconveniences were just “Champagne problems” for the winners in life—because, if given the choice, who wouldn't want to be smart, good-looking, charismatic, athletic, and wealthy? Just like the small dark dots within Yin and Yang, the existence of opposites is real—but I believed they made up only a tiny part of the good that people experience.

Recently, I met someone who challenged that belief. (The story of how we met is complicated—a story for another day.) On paper, she is perfect—extremely smart, athletic, good-looking, and able to pick up just about anything (truly anything) with ease. She's knowledgeable, kind, has a great personality, excels academically, has a promising career, and checks every box you could imagine in an ideal person. And yet, she still struggles.

Her experience made me turn inward and reflect on my own. Over the years, I've worked hard to achieve accomplishments that others would consider impressive. But even as I appear more “successful” on paper, my internal struggles haven't lessened. This doesn't quite make sense to me—if I'm collecting more “greatness” than “negatives,” shouldn't life be getting easier, instead of stagnating or even becoming harder?

Throughout college, I occasionally felt overwhelmed—burned out, depressed, and lonely. These feelings came in unpredictable waves. During those times, I lost motivation to keep up with my daily routine, and even simple tasks, like eating or saying hi to a friend on the street, felt impossible. I wished I had the willpower to push through, to force myself back into motion—but more often than not, I couldn't. I wished I were stronger—strong enough to suppress the melancholy.

I shared my story with her, and, surprisingly, she told me she feels the same way. She, too, has moments where she feels overwhelmed by sadness and loneliness. But unlike me, she has an extraordinary ability to push through. She hides her struggles so well that no one would ever suspect a thing. She goes to the gym, finishes her work, studies, hangs out with friends—all while hiding her struggles behind a smile.

And yet, she admitted that it's exhausting. The sadness is always there—just hidden. But not gone.

Her story made me rethink my own experience. During my low points, I often had to reach out for help—or it was simply obvious to those around me that I wasn't okay. My family and friends would step in, checking in, helping me find my way back. Not being “strong enough” actually gave me the chance to face and begin working through my problems—with support.

I began to wonder—maybe the strength I envied wasn't strength at all. Or maybe it was, but not the kind I was looking for. Trying to become mentally tougher wouldn't solve my problems—it would only bury them until they eventually resurface, stronger than before. I used to think strength meant suppression. Now I realize that might be exactly the problem.

Being good at something is a blessing—but it can also be a curse. It makes you and everyone around you ignore the shadow that comes with the greatness: how can someone so popular struggle with friendships and loneliness? How can someone so brilliant feel crushed by expectations? On the other hand, when we struggle to make friends or feel disconnected, we often think becoming more popular will solve the problem. When we feel crushed by others' expectations, we convince ourselves that being smarter or more accomplished is the answer. But these efforts rarely heal the root issue. Instead, they just push the problem deeper, covering it up without addressing the problem beneath.

Like Yin and Yang, struggle and success don't cancel each other out—they coexist. As I grow in one area, the shadows grow too. What hasn't changed is my capacity to endure them.

In the future, instead of believing that becoming “better” will make my struggles disappear, I want to slow down and take time to understand them. That might mean I improve more slowly—at least on paper, or by other people's standards—but I know it will lead to something more sustainable. After all, what could be more empowering than facing your fears head-on and learning to grow with them?
