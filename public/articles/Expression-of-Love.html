Expression of Love

February 11th, 2025

Reading time: 3 minutes

Back in 2018, during my college application process, my friend Alex suggested I read "50 Successful Harvard Application Essays," a compilation of personal statements from students admitted to Harvard, to help with my challenges in writing college essays. One essay, in particular, left a deep impression on me, even after many years. The author wrote about how, unlike her non-Asian friends' families, verbal expressions of 'love' were rare in her Chinese family. Instead of saying 'I love you,' her parents would show love through acts of service: her mom staying up late to support her during those late-night study grinds, bringing her cut watermelon at 3 a.m., and her dad waiting in the 100ºF heat to pick her up from school every day.

I resonated with her essay, as my parents do the same. When my mom misses me, which she is too shy to ever admit, she drives an hour just to buy my favorite snack from my favorite bakery. Instead of saying 'I love you,' she emphasizes that she waited under the sun for an hour just for me. My dad, on the other hand, rarely talks about his efforts for me. It is only through my mom that I know he often puts aside his work and important meetings just to drive me, so he could spend more time with me. Growing up, I equated love with acts of service, hardship, and sacrifice. Though this understanding sometimes felt odd, I also took pride in grasping its depth.

The shyness of Chinese people in expressing love, coupled with the notion that love equals sacrifice, is also evident in pop culture. In Chinese love songs, the word 'love' rarely appears in the lyrics, let alone the entire phrase 'I love you.' For example, in classic love songs like “至少還有你 "and “輸了你贏了世界又如何,” the word 'love' is never mentioned throughout the entire song; instead, they talk about how they would give up the entire world just for another person. In one of his comedy skits, Jimmy O. Yang talked about a time when he was high on weed and called his mom to tell her he loved her. His mom was so shocked that she thought he was dying from cancer. He says that in his family, they show love by serving fruits and giving red envelopes. Even on social media, people express love by showcasing the gifts and effort they put into their relationships. It often seems like love is measured by how much you sacrifice—saving up months of salary for an expensive gift or treating your partner to a lavish meal just to prove your love.

Last year, I ended my first long-term relationship. Since then, I've been contemplating and reading various books to understand what love truly means. Recently, I came across the book 單車失竊記 by Wu Ming-Yi, which has easily become my second favorite book of all time. It helped me understand the source of my odd feelings. The author describes how his mom expresses 'love' by recounting all the hardships she endured for him, which makes him feel both grateful and embarrassed. The realization struck me: it's hard to feel happy when someone suffers for you. Moreover, when love is equated with suffering, sacrifice, and hardship, loving someone doesn't seem delightful. When love is tied with hardship, the phrase 'I love you' transforms from a sweet expression to a heavy and burdensome declaration. Hardship and sacrifice are the evidence of love, and love is the result of hardship and sacrifice.

Understanding love as sacrifice has shaped me, but I now wonder—can love exist without obligation? Can it be freely given, pure, with no expectation? If so, how can we measure and feel love? I don’t have the answer yet, but if love can truly exist without obligation, perhaps it must be built on trust and unwavering confidence. And maybe, when love no longer needs to be proven, that is when it is truly pure.
