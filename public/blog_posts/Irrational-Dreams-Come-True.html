Irrational Dreams Come True

June 21st, 2026

Reading time: 7 minute

We are made of fragments of memory.

In the summer of 2009, when I was eight years old, my parents signed me up for a 21-day trip to Orlando, Florida, with about 20 other kids from my school. The oldest kid on the trip was only eleven.

For the first two weeks, we attended summer camps at the Orlando Science Center and NASA, spending one week at each. During the final week, we went to Disney World and Universal Studios. We were taken care of by two adults and a bus driver. Every day, they would drop us off at the entrance of the park, say goodbye, and then 20-plus Taiwanese kids would rush through the gates like little minions, taking over the most magical place on earth.

To be honest, I do not remember most of what happened on that trip. What I do remember is feeling shy because I did not understand most of what people were saying, but also excited and happy at the same time. For most of my life, I remembered Disney as a fun amusement park and nothing more.

However, there is one incident from that trip that I still remember vividly to this day.

I was weaving my way through the crowd at Animal Kingdom with three other friends when, all of a sudden, an Asian woman stopped us. She squatted down so she could talk to us at eye level. For reference, I was about 140 centimeters (or 4'7") tall at the time, and my friends were around the same height.

She gently asked us where we were from. After we told her we were from Taiwan, she smiled and told us that she was also Taiwanese. Then she asked why we were not accompanied by an adult and whether we could contact our guardians. We did not have phones back then, but we showed her the cards we carried with all of our emergency contact information on them.

I still remember watching her stand back up and speak to her friends in fluent English — at least, it sounded fluent to me. She told them they should go ahead without her because she wanted to make sure we were safe.

She then took us to the front gate to meet our driver. When we found him, she told him firmly that it was not okay to leave children alone in the park. After that, she squatted down again and gently told us to stay safe and have fun while we were in the United States.

Then she left.

Maybe I remember her because I thought she was pretty. Or maybe I remember her because, even at eight years old, I saw in her so many qualities I admired. She spoke English confidently. She stood up for us. She was gentle when she talked to us, but strong when she needed to speak to an adult. That moment left a deep impression on me.

Seventeen years later, my college friend Eugene told me I should visit him at Disney World.

After graduating from Berkeley with a degree in EECS, Eugene had done internships in Livermore, upstate New York, and Texas. At the time, he was working temporarily at Disney World as a Star Wars cast member before joining the Air Force a few months later.

I had not seen Eugene in a long time, and I was also in the middle of switching jobs, which gave me a rare window of time off. So, naturally, after he agreed to give me free tickets to the park, I agreed to visit him in Orlando, seventeen years after my first trip there.

While wandering through Disney World, trying to pick up little pieces of memory from 17 years ago, I asked Eugene a question I had always wanted to ask him. With his EECS degree from Berkeley and his intelligence, he could easily have found a high-paying job in big tech. Why didn't he do that?

He replied, "Ew. I don't want that."

I laughed, but I did not really understand his answer.

Last year, after my brother's graduation trip in Boston, I had a conversation with Ellis. She told me that she thought I tended to find a reason for everything — to rationalize things, to build a theory around everything, to explain every decision with logic. She said it in a neutral, maybe even positive, way, and I agreed with her. Growing up, I have always been a rational person. I like planning before making decisions. I like analyzing things. I like challenging what I believe and what other people believe.

But when she said it, I still felt awkwardly embarrassed.

I did not think she was wrong. I did not even think it was a bad thing. But for some reason, hearing it out loud made me uncomfortable.

Today was my last day at Disney World. I saw a Fantasmic plushy in a store at Hollywood Studios. Suddenly, so many memories from my childhood came back, especially memories of being at home with my mom. My mom had kept the exact same plushy on her bedside table for as long as I could remember — from when I was born until we moved to a new house after I left for college in the United States. Until I saw it in the store, I had not realized that I had not seen that plushy since we moved to the new house.

I never thought that an object I had barely paid attention to could bring back so many memories.

I stayed at the park until 10:30 p.m. to watch Fantasmic, even though I had not planned to at first. The show was great, so much better than I imagined a water show could be. Toward the end, with the singing, the music, and the dancing, I saw a mermaid dancing with her prince and waving at the audience. For some reason, I suddenly felt touched.

For a moment, I understood why Disney is called the place where dreams come true.

When I was eight, I saw Disney as a park with fun rides. At 25, I first saw it as a place to hang out with friends. But in that moment, I understood why people love Disney, and why it leaves a soft spot in their hearts.

After the show, I suddenly thought about Eugene. Maybe, many years from now, it would be nice for him to look back and remember that, for a few months, he worked as one of his favorite characters in a land of magic, making people happy. Maybe that memory would mean something to him in a way that no salary, title, or career path could fully explain.

And suddenly, I understood why I felt embarrassed after hearing Ellis's words. Deep inside, maybe I don't want to be so logical and practical. Maybe I have only used my rational side as a disguise — to hide that I don't have the courage to do things that don't make sense to people. Perhaps not everything has to make sense in a traditional way. Not everything has to be practical. Not everything has to be structured, just like this essay. Maybe a meaningful, well-lived life doesn't have to be carefully planned and executed to meet society's standards.

As I write this, more thoughts keep coming, and I don't plan to revise this essay after I wrap up. And that's okay. I feel good posting this now, and maybe in a couple of years, I'll look back and find some fond memories of today.
